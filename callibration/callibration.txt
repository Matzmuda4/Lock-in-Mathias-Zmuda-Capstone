When the sunlight strikes raindrops in the air, they act as a prism and form a rainbow. The rainbow is a division of white light into many beautiful colors. These take the shape of a long round arch, with its path high above, and its two ends apparently beyond the horizon. There is, according to legend, a boiling pot of gold at one end. People look, but no one ever finds it.

When a man looks for something beyond his reach, his friends say he is looking for the pot of gold at the end of the rainbow. Throughout the centuries people have explained the rainbow in various ways. Some have accepted it as a miracle without physical explanation. To the Hebrews it was a token that there would be no more universal floods. The Greeks used to imagine that it was a sign from the gods to foretell war or heavy rain. The Norsemen considered the rainbow as a bridge over which the gods passed from earth to their home in the sky. Others have tried to explain the phenomenon physically. Aristotle thought that the rainbow was caused by reflection of the sun's rays by the rain.

Since then physicists have found that it is not reflection, but refraction by the raindrops which causes the rainbows. Many complicated ideas about the rainbow have been formed. The difference in the rainbow depends considerably upon the size of the drops, and the width of the colored band increases as the size of the drops increases.

The actual primary rainbow observed is said to be the effect of super-imposition of a number of bows. If the red of the second bow falls upon the green of the first, the result is to give a bow with an abnormally wide yellow band, since red and green light when mixed form yellow. This is a very common type of bow, one showing mainly red and yellow, with little or no green or blue.

Sometimes a second, fainter rainbow appears outside the primary one. This is called a secondary rainbow, and its colors are reversed, with red on the inner edge and violet on the outer edge. The space between the primary and secondary bows often looks darker than the rest of the sky. This darker band is known as Alexander's band, and it occurs because less light is scattered toward the observer from that region when the rays undergo different numbers of internal reflections inside the raindrops. Under special conditions, people may also see very faint extra bands inside the primary rainbow. These are called supernumerary rainbows, and they appear as closely spaced, pastel-like rings. They are most easily observed when the raindrops are very small and nearly uniform in size, such as in a fine mist. Although they look delicate, these extra bands show that the rainbow depends not only on simple refraction and reflection, but also on the way light waves interact and overlap as they leave the droplets.

Rainbows can only be seen when the sun is at a relatively low angle in the sky, typically below 42 degrees above the horizon. This is why they are most commonly observed in the early morning or late afternoon, when the sun is close to the horizon and rain is falling in the opposite direction. The full arc of a rainbow is actually a complete circle, but because the ground interrupts it, observers at the surface can only see the upper half. From an aircraft, however, it is sometimes possible to see a rainbow as a full circular band of color suspended in the air below. The center of this circle is always located at the antisolar point, the point in the sky directly opposite the sun from the observer's position.

The order of colors in a rainbow follows a consistent sequence from outer to inner edge: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, and violet. This sequence reflects the way different wavelengths of visible light are bent by different amounts as they pass through water droplets. Red light, which has the longest wavelength in the visible spectrum, is refracted the least and appears at the top of the arc. Violet light, with the shortest wavelength, bends the most and appears at the bottom. The width and brightness of each color band can vary depending on the size and uniformity of the raindrops producing the effect.

Not all rainbows are formed by rain. A moonbow, also called a lunar rainbow, is produced by moonlight rather than sunlight passing through droplets of water. Because moonlight is far dimmer than sunlight, moonbows appear much fainter and are often seen only as pale arcs by the naked eye. Long-exposure photography, however, can reveal the full spectrum of colors in a moonbow. Similarly, fogbows form in fog or mist where the droplets are so small that they produce very little color separation, resulting in a broad, pale arc with only faint traces of red on the outer edge and blue on the inner.

Throughout human history, the rainbow has carried deep symbolic meaning across many cultures and traditions. In many traditions it represents a bridge between the earthly and the divine, a sign of hope or renewal after hardship. In Norse mythology, the Bifrost bridge was said to be a rainbow connecting the world of humans to the realm of the gods. In the Biblical tradition, the rainbow appeared after the great flood as a covenant of peace. Modern science has done nothing to diminish the sense of wonder the rainbow inspires. If anything, understanding the precise physics of refraction, reflection, and wave interference that conspire to paint those curved bands of color across a stormy sky makes the sight all the more remarkable.
